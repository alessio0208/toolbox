<h2 id="mp-tfa-h2" style="margin:0.5em; background:#cef2e0; font-family:inherit; font-size:120%; font-weight:bold; border:1px solid #a3bfb1; color:#000; padding:0.2em 0.4em;"><span id="From_today.27s_featured_article"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="From_today's_featured_article">From today's featured article</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfa" style="padding:0.1em 0.6em;">
<div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Banksia_aculeata.JPG" class="image" title="Banksia aculeata"><img alt="Banksia aculeata" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Banksia_aculeata.JPG/100px-Banksia_aculeata.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Banksia_aculeata.JPG/150px-Banksia_aculeata.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Banksia_aculeata.JPG/200px-Banksia_aculeata.JPG 2x" data-file-width="683" data-file-height="1024" height="150" width="100"></a></div>
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Banksia_aculeata" title="Banksia aculeata">Banksia aculeata</a></b></i>, the prickly banksia, is a plant of the family <a href="/wiki/Proteaceae" title="Proteaceae">Proteaceae</a> native to the <a href="/wiki/Stirling_Range" title="Stirling Range">Stirling Range</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southwest_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Southwest Australia">southwest</a> of <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. A bushy shrub up to 2&nbsp;m (7&nbsp;ft) tall, it has fissured grey bark on its trunk and branches, and dense foliage and leaves with very prickly <a href="/wiki/Serration" title="Serration">serrated</a> margins. Its unusual pinkish, <a href="/wiki/Pendent" title="Pendent">pendent</a> (hanging) flower spikes, known as <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescences</a>, are generally hidden in the foliage and appear during the early summer. Unlike many other banksia species, it does not have a woody base, or <a href="/wiki/Lignotuber" title="Lignotuber">lignotuber</a>. Although it was collected in the 1840s by the naturalist <a href="/wiki/James_Drummond_(botanist)" title="James Drummond (botanist)">James Drummond</a>, it was not <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">formally described</a> until 1981, in <a href="/wiki/Alex_George" title="Alex George">Alex George</a>'s monograph of the genus. A rare plant, <i>B. aculeata</i> is found in gravelly soils in elevated areas. Native to a habitat burnt by periodic <a href="/wiki/Bushfires_in_Australia" title="Bushfires in Australia">bushfires</a>, it is killed by fire and regenerates from seed afterwards. In contrast to other Western Australian banksias, it appears to have some resistance to <i><a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_cinnamomi" title="Phytophthora cinnamomi">Phytophthora cinnamomi</a></i>, a soil-borne <a href="/wiki/Water_mould" class="mw-redirect" title="Water mould">water mould</a>. (<a href="/wiki/Banksia_aculeata" title="Banksia aculeata"><b>Full&nbsp;article...</b></a>)</p>

<h2 id="mp-tfp-h2" style="margin:0.5em; background:#ddcef2; font-family:inherit; font-size:120%; font-weight:bold; border:1px solid #afa3bf; color:#000; padding:0.2em 0.4em"><span id="Today.27s_featured_picture"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Today's_featured_picture">Today's featured picture</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfp" style="margin:0.1em 0.4em 0.6em;">
<table role="presentation" style="margin:0 3px 3px; width:100%; text-align:left; background-color:transparent; border-collapse: collapse;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="padding:0 0.9em 0 0;"><a href="/wiki/File:Taraxacum_officinale_(Slovenia).jpg" class="image" title="Taraxacum officinale"><img alt="Taraxacum officinale" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Taraxacum_officinale_%28Slovenia%29.jpg/320px-Taraxacum_officinale_%28Slovenia%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Taraxacum_officinale_%28Slovenia%29.jpg/480px-Taraxacum_officinale_%28Slovenia%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Taraxacum_officinale_%28Slovenia%29.jpg/640px-Taraxacum_officinale_%28Slovenia%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1995" data-file-height="1995" height="320" width="320"></a></td>
<td style="padding:0 6px 0 0">
<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Taraxacum_officinale" title="Taraxacum officinale">Taraxacum officinale</a></b></i> is a flowering <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">herbaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial plant</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">Asteraceae</a> (Compositae). Commonly known as the dandelion, it can be found growing in <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate</a> regions of the world, in lawns, on roadsides, on disturbed banks and shores of waterways, and other areas with moist soils. Although widely considered a weed, especially in lawns and along roadsides, it is sometimes used as a <a href="/wiki/Herbalism" title="Herbalism">medical herb</a> and in food preparation. The species is well known for its yellow flower heads that turn into round balls of silver tufted fruits that <a href="/wiki/Seed_dispersal" title="Seed dispersal">disperse</a> in the wind.</p>
